Residential cleaning offers some of the strongest recurring revenue in home services, but buyers constantly misunderstand the model.

The core challenge isn't finding houses to clean. Your core challenge is finding reliable people to clean them. 🧵

When you buy a residential cleaning franchise, you aren't really in the cleaning business. You are buying a recruiting and staffing company.

The business scales exactly as fast as your hiring funnel.

Demand is rarely the issue. Turnover is.

This is why the franchise model wins here. You get established recruiting systems, training playbooks, and routing software that takes years to refine on your own. 

You control the margin, but you have to treat your cleaners as your primary customers.

Most franchisors in this space mandate an employee (W-2) model over relying on independent contractors (1099). 

Why? Control. When the brand's reputation rests on consistency, you want full control over uniforms, training, and client experience.

It's asset-light. You don't need a retail buildout. You can launch from a flex space or home office and scale vehicles only as the team grows.

But asset-light does not mean hands-off.

If you expect to run a maid service absentee from day one, look at a different category. The first few years require intense operational focus to build the initial teams. 

Only once you hit scale can you layer in a general manager.

If you can manage people and run a hiring funnel, the recurring revenue in cleaning is hard to beat. Just know what business you're actually buying.

## Article: Staffing Franchises: Margin Structure and B2B Revenue
**Slug:** `staffing-franchises`

### Twitter/X Thread

B2B staffing is one of the most reliable routes to recurring revenue.

But success depends entirely on understanding the difference between temporary placement and direct hire. 🧵

Temporary placement is the volume game. A client needs 20 workers for 3 months. You deploy them. 

You pay the worker X, the client pays you Y. The margin in between is your revenue.

It's recurring revenue, but the cash flow dynamic is brutal if you aren't prepared.

You have to pay your temp workers every week. Your B2B client might pay the invoice in 30 to 60 days. 

That creates a massive working capital gap.

This is why the franchise model wins in staffing. The best franchisors finance the payroll for you. 

They act as the back office, funding the gap in exchange for a fee. That removes the biggest risk of bankruptcy from over-scaling.

Direct hire is the opposite. It's a low-volume, high-margin game.

You place a permanent executive, and the client pays you a fee matching a percentage of their salary. One placement can generate huge revenue.

You are buying an operating system, compliance frameworks, and back-office support.

But you have to bring the sales. If you excel at B2B networking, the scale potential here is significant.

## Article: Garage Transformation Franchises: High-Ticket, Project-Based Service
**Slug:** `garage-transformation-franchises`

### Twitter/X Thread

When evaluating home services, most buyers default to recurring revenue like pest control or cleaning. 

But high-ticket, project-based models like garage transformation offer a completely different margin structure. 🧵

You are not selling a commodity. You are selling a premium home improvement. 

Homeowners increasingly view the garage as an extension of their living space.

Because project sizes range from $3,000 to over $10,000, you don't need thousands of customers. 

A single crew installing two floors a week generates significant top-line revenue.

But project-based means you start at zero every month. 

When a floor is done, it's done for twenty years. The business runs almost entirely on your ability to generate constant net-new leads.

Lead generation and consumer sales are the core of this business. 

You must be comfortable visiting a home, demonstrating samples, and closing a multi-thousand dollar contract.

The operational side is precision-driven. 

If concrete is prepped wrong, the floor fails. A failed floor means doing it over, which destroys the profit margin for that job and damages your local reputation.

This is not a business you can run remotely from day one. You have to inspect job sites, ensure quality standards, and manage crew retention.

It's asset-light, but you still need a flex space or warehouse to store grinding machines, heavy equipment, and hazardous materials.

If you don't mind the constant sales engine, the high margins in garage transformation outperform many high-volume service concepts.
