### Big-Name vs. Emerging: Which Franchise to Buy
**URL:** `/resources/big-name-vs-emerging-which-franchise-to-buy`

**Twitter/X Thread:**
```
"Should I buy a household-name franchise or an emerging one?"

Most people assume the big name is safer.

It's not that simple. 🧵

What the big names have:

Brand recognition that opens doors. Customers who already know the product. Proven systems that have been refined over thousands of units.

What they don't have: available territory.

The best markets for Dunkin', McDonald's, and Anytime Fitness were claimed years ago. If a prime territory opens up, the resale price reflects it.

What emerging brands have:

Available territory — including the markets where growth is actually happening.

Lower entry cost in many cases.

The upside of a brand going from 50 units to 500 on your watch.

And the risk:

Emerging brands have shorter track records. Thinner system support. Less public data on unit performance.

The right framework:

I look for what I call triple-A brands. Established enough to have real systems and real support. Not so established that the best territories are gone.

Typically this is $5M+ in system revenue, 50–200 units, 3+ years in business, and an FDD that discloses actual Item 19 data.

That's the sweet spot.

### Red Flags: Franchise Types to Avoid
**URL:** `/resources/red-flags-franchise-types-to-avoid`

**Twitter/X Thread:**
```
Not all franchises are worth buying.

Here are the patterns I watch for — and what they signal. 🧵

Red flag 1: No Item 19 disclosure

Franchisors aren't required to disclose financial performance data. Some choose not to.

That alone is not disqualifying. But if a brand can't or won't show you unit economics, you're flying blind on the most important number in the decision.

What to do: ask for earnings claims from franchisees directly. If the brand discourages that conversation, stop.

Red flag 2: Closure rates that don't add up

Item 20 has system-wide unit counts: how many opened, how many closed.

A high closure rate is obvious. But closing isn't the only way units disappear. Franchisors can buy out struggling owners before a formal termination. The raw closure number understates what's happening at the bottom.

Look for retention data: of the owners who opened 3 years ago, how many are still operating?

Red flag 3: Litigation from franchisees specifically

Item 3 has the litigation history. Claims from third parties are different from claims from franchisees.

A pattern of franchisee lawsuits tells you something about what it's like to be in business with this franchisor.

Red flag 4: Recent ownership change at the franchisor level

Private equity acquisitions have changed the franchise landscape. Some PE-backed brands maintain strong franchisee support. Others immediately cut support costs and focus on unit count growth.

If leadership changed recently, ask who made the decision to enter franchising and who is running support now.

Full piece: [link]
```

### Fast-Growing Franchise Brand: Good Sign or Red Flag?
**URL:** `/resources/fast-growing-franchise-brand-good-sign-or-red-flag`

**Twitter/X Thread:**
```
Fast growth in a franchise system can mean two completely different things.

Knowing the difference before you buy in matters a lot. 🧵

Growth can indicate:

Genuine demand for the concept. Strong franchisee economics. A support infrastructure that's actually delivering. A brand that's winning.

Or it can indicate:

A sales-focused development team moving units faster than the support system can absorb them. Territory saturation happening ahead of a slowdown. Bad deals being sold to undercapitalized buyers.

The tells:

Franchisee satisfaction — FBR (Franchise Business Review) publishes satisfaction scores from franchisees. If a fast-growing brand has low franchisee satisfaction, that's your answer.

Support-to-franchise ratio — how many support staff per franchisee? Growing 50% in units should come with a proportional investment in support.

Item 20 retention — are early franchisees still operating? Fast-growing brands that are quietly churning through unit owners are a different story than brands growing with stable, profitable franchisees.

Item 19 trajectory — are unit economics holding as the system scales? Or are averages being dragged down by newer, struggling locations?

Growth is a signal. It's not the signal.

Does this franchisor actually care about franchisee success?

The answer isn't in the pitch. It's in the evidence.

Evidence 1: What does support look like after the check clears?

Not during due diligence. After.

A franchisor that takes anyone with a check is a franchisor that doesn't need you to succeed to run their business.

A franchisor that turns away undercapitalized or profile-mismatched candidates cares about unit performance, not just unit count.

### How to Pick a Franchise Territory
**URL:** `/resources/how-to-pick-a-franchise-territory`

**Twitter/X Thread:**
```
Territory selection is one of the most underestimated decisions in franchise ownership.

Pick wrong and no amount of effort makes it right. Here's how to think through it. 🧵

The first question: what does your franchise model actually need?

B2C concepts need the right customer demographic. A premium home services brand needs household income to support premium pricing. A youth-focused concept needs the right age distribution.

B2B concepts need the right business density. Commercial clients, industrial areas, or a certain SIC code distribution that's worth mapping.

Know what your model requires before you evaluate a territory.

The second question: is the territory actually available?

Check before you get emotionally attached.

And if it's "available," understand what available means. Some brands have partial territory availability — they sold an adjacent market and the encroachment risk is real.

The third question: what does 5-year growth look like?

A territory that looks mediocre today may be the right call if the right demographic trends are moving there.

Conversely, a territory that looks prime today may be declining in the metrics that matter for your specific concept.

Use data sources the franchisor doesn't provide: Census data, local economic development reports, real estate market trends.

The fourth question: how many units can this territory support?

If multi-unit is your goal, can the territory support unit 2 and unit 3 without cannibalizing unit 1?

### The Franchise Agreement: What You Can and Can't Negotiate
**URL:** `/resources/the-franchise-agreement-what-you-can-and-cant-negotiate`

**Twitter/X Thread:**
```
The franchise agreement is the document that governs your relationship with the franchisor for the next 10 years.

Most candidates think they can't negotiate it at all. Most franchisors say exactly the same.

The truth is somewhere in the middle. 🧵

What you actually can't negotiate:

Brand standards. Fee structures. Territory definitions. Renewal terms. Transfer fees. Most operational requirements.

These are in the agreement because consistency across the system is what makes the franchise worth buying in the first place. Franchisors don't move on these.

What you sometimes can negotiate:

Development timeline — if you're opening multiple units, the timeline for unit 2 and 3 is sometimes flexible depending on the brand's development goals in your area.

Specific operational waivers — market-specific modifications that don't affect system standards (e.g., product sourcing exceptions for regional availability issues).

Personal guarantee terms — the guarantor structure and what it attaches to is sometimes a conversation.

Initial term conditions — if something was represented verbally, get it written into the agreement as a condition.

The most important thing:

Have a franchise attorney review the agreement. Not a general business attorney — someone who specializes in franchise law and has read hundreds of these documents.

The legal cost is $2K–$5K. The agreement runs 10 years. The math is obvious.

Full piece: [link]
```

The W2 gets old. You start looking at options. You find a franchise that excites you. You leave, own it, build freedom.

When you're actually ready:

You can answer all three clearly. You've done enough research to know the difference between a good concept and a marketing pitch. And you're in a position to make a $150K–$400K decision after considered analysis, not under financial pressure.

### What Is Your Time Worth: The ROI Math of Franchise Ownership
**URL:** `/resources/what-is-your-time-worth-the-roi-math-of-franchise-ownership`

**Twitter/X Thread:**
```
Most people evaluate franchises on investment and revenue.

The variable most people don't put into the math: their own time.

Here's how to actually run the ROI calculation. 🧵

The franchise is not a passive investment. It is a business.

You are trading capital + time for returns.

The return calculation needs to include what your time is worth — or the math is wrong.

Start with your W2 equivalent:

If you're making $150K in your current job, that's your baseline. The franchise needs to pay you at least that much in a management salary equivalent — plus return on your capital — to actually be an improvement.

If it can't, you're working harder for less.

The real calculation:

Year 1–2 returns are typically reinvested in growth, capital repayment, and operational stability. They're not the measure.

Years 3–5 are when the model shows you what it's capable of. That's when the ROI math becomes meaningful.

What you're really buying:

Not a salary. Not even a return. You're buying the right to build an asset with a multiple.

A franchise with $200K annual earnings that sells at a 2.5x multiple is a $500K sale event, on top of the salary you took from it for years.

That's the full picture. Model it before you evaluate a brand.

### Your First 90 Days as a Franchise Owner
**URL:** `/resources/your-first-90-days-as-a-franchise-owner`

**Twitter/X Thread:**
```
The first 90 days of franchise ownership are almost universally harder than buyers expect.

Not because the franchise is a bad investment. Because almost nobody tells you what to actually focus on.

Here's what matters most. 🧵

Day 1 through 30: your only job is to learn the system.

Not improve the system. Not prove you're different. Learn the system exactly as designed.

The instinct to customize is strong, especially for people coming from corporate leadership where initiative was rewarded.

In a franchise, the system exists because someone already tried the creative approach. Trust the playbook until you understand why every part of it is there.

Days 30 through 60: hire, or find out your hiring plan doesn't work.

The quality of your first hire is the most important variable in your first two years of operation.

Move faster on this than feels comfortable. A mediocre hire that delays your open is worse than an imperfect hire you can train.

Days 60 through 90: fill the pipeline.

Marketing in the first 90 days determines your revenue 90 days from now. This is not the moment to spend conservatively.

Follow the franchisor's launch marketing playbook. Don't negotiate with it.

What not to worry about yet:

Profitability. Optimization. Process refinement. All of that is future-Kelsey's problem. Right now, you're learning, hiring, and filling the pipeline.

Nobody who owns a successful junk removal franchise loves junk removal.

Nobody who owns a successful commercial cleaning franchise has a deep passion for cleaning.

They love:

Running a business. Managing people. Building systems. The financial freedom that comes from strong unit economics.

The franchise is the vehicle. Your love for the vehicle is irrelevant.

What actually predicts success:

Fit between your working style and the operating model.

Your enjoyment of whatever the business does is secondary.

The question isn't "could I see myself doing this?"

The question is "does the role I play in this business match how I actually work well?"

### One Unit or Multi-Unit: What First-Timers Get Wrong
**URL:** `/resources/one-unit-or-multi-unit-what-first-timers-get-wrong`

**Twitter/X Thread:**
```
The mistake I see most often from first-time franchise buyers:

Buying a multi-unit deal before they understand what they're getting into.

Here's what first-timers get wrong — and what the right sequence looks like. 🧵

The appeal of multi-unit is obvious:

More locations means more revenue. Buying multiple territories at once usually costs less per territory than buying them sequentially. The math looks better on paper.

Here's what the math doesn't show:

Multi-unit from day one means running multiple units before you know how to run one.

The mistakes you make in unit one — hiring, training, systems, cash flow management — you're making simultaneously across multiple locations. Each mistake costs more.

The right sequence for most buyers:

Buy one. Learn it. Get it to positive cash flow. Understand where your model actually works and where it doesn't. Then buy the second one with real data.

When multi-unit from the start actually makes sense:

When you're buying into a brand designed explicitly for portfolio ownership from the beginning. Some franchise models are built for this. When the territories are contiguous and the operational model shares infrastructure efficiently. When you have the capital cushion to absorb a longer, more complicated ramp.

Even then — know what you're doing.
