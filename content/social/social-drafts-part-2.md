### Red Flags: Franchise Types to Avoid
**URL:** `/resources/red-flags-franchise-types-to-avoid`

**Twitter/X Thread:**
```
Not all franchises are worth buying.

Here are the patterns I watch for — and what they signal. 🧵

Red flag 1: No Item 19 disclosure

Franchisors aren't required to disclose financial performance data. Some choose not to.

That alone is not disqualifying. But if a brand can't or won't show you unit economics, you're flying blind on the most important number in the decision.

What to do: ask for earnings claims from franchisees directly. If the brand discourages that conversation, stop.

Red flag 2: Closure rates that don't add up

Item 20 has system-wide unit counts: how many opened, how many closed.

A high closure rate is obvious. But closing isn't the only way units disappear. Franchisors can buy out struggling owners before a formal termination. The raw closure number understates what's happening at the bottom.

Look for retention data: of the owners who opened 3 years ago, how many are still operating?

Red flag 3: Litigation from franchisees specifically

Item 3 has the litigation history. Claims from third parties are different from claims from franchisees.

A pattern of franchisee lawsuits tells you something about what it's like to be in business with this franchisor.

Red flag 4: Recent ownership change at the franchisor level

Private equity acquisitions have changed the franchise landscape. Some PE-backed brands maintain strong franchisee support. Others immediately cut support costs and focus on unit count growth.

If leadership changed recently, ask who made the decision to enter franchising and who is running support now.
```

```
A sales-focused development team moving units faster than the support system can absorb them. Territory saturation happening ahead of a slowdown. Bad deals being sold to undercapitalized buyers.

Franchisee satisfaction — FBR (Franchise Business Review) publishes satisfaction scores from franchisees. If a fast-growing brand has low franchisee satisfaction, that's your answer.

Growth is a signal. It's not the signal.
```

### How to Pick a Franchise Territory
**URL:** `/resources/how-to-pick-a-franchise-territory`

**Twitter/X Thread:**
```
Territory selection is one of the most underestimated decisions in franchise ownership.

Pick wrong and no amount of effort makes it right. Here's how to think through it. 🧵

The first question: what does your franchise model actually need?

B2C concepts need the right customer demographic. A premium home services brand needs household income to support premium pricing. A youth-focused concept needs the right age distribution.

B2B concepts need the right business density. Commercial clients, industrial areas, or a certain SIC code distribution that's worth mapping.

Know what your model requires before you evaluate a territory.

The second question: is the territory actually available?

Check before you get emotionally attached.

And if it's "available," understand what available means. Some brands have partial territory availability — they sold an adjacent market and the encroachment risk is real.

The third question: what does 5-year growth look like?

A territory that looks mediocre today may be the right call if the right demographic trends are moving there.

Conversely, a territory that looks prime today may be declining in the metrics that matter for your specific concept.

Use data sources the franchisor doesn't provide: Census data, local economic development reports, real estate market trends.

The fourth question: how many units can this territory support?

If multi-unit is your goal, can the territory support unit 2 and unit 3 without cannibalizing unit 1?
```

### The Franchise Agreement: What You Can and Can't Negotiate
**URL:** `/resources/the-franchise-agreement-what-you-can-and-cant-negotiate`

**Twitter/X Thread:**
```
The franchise agreement is the document that governs your relationship with the franchisor for the next 10 years.

Most candidates think they can't negotiate it at all. Most franchisors say exactly the same.

The truth is somewhere in the middle. 🧵

What you actually can't negotiate:

Brand standards. Fee structures. Territory definitions. Renewal terms. Transfer fees. Most operational requirements.

These are in the agreement because consistency across the system is what makes the franchise worth buying in the first place. Franchisors don't move on these.

What you sometimes can negotiate:

Development timeline — if you're opening multiple units, the timeline for unit 2 and 3 is sometimes flexible depending on the brand's development goals in your area.

Specific operational waivers — market-specific modifications that don't affect system standards (e.g., product sourcing exceptions for regional availability issues).

Personal guarantee terms — the guarantor structure and what it attaches to is sometimes a conversation.

Initial term conditions — if something was represented verbally, get it written into the agreement as a condition.

The most important thing:

Have a franchise attorney review the agreement. Not a general business attorney — someone who specializes in franchise law and has read hundreds of these documents.

The legal cost is $2K–$5K. The agreement runs 10 years. The math is obvious.
```

```
Not a salary. Not even a return. You're buying the right to build an asset with a multiple.
```

```
The first 90 days of franchise ownership are almost universally harder than buyers expect.

Not because the franchise is a bad investment. Because almost nobody tells you what to actually focus on.

Here's what matters most. 🧵

Day 1 through 30: your only job is to learn the system.

Not improve the system. Not prove you're different. Learn the system exactly as designed.

The instinct to customize is strong, especially for people coming from corporate leadership where initiative was rewarded.

In a franchise, the system exists because someone already tried the creative approach. Trust the playbook until you understand why every part of it is there.

Days 60 through 90: fill the pipeline.

Marketing in the first 90 days determines your revenue 90 days from now. This is not the moment to spend conservatively.

Follow the franchisor's launch marketing playbook. Don't negotiate with it.

What not to worry about yet:

Profitability. Optimization. Process refinement. All of that is future-Kelsey's problem. Right now, you're learning, hiring, and filling the pipeline.
```

### SBA Loan vs. ROBS: Which Franchise Funding Path Fits You
**URL:** `/resources/sba-loan-vs-robs-franchise-funding-comparison`

**Twitter/X Thread:**
```
Two paths dominate franchise funding.

SBA loans. ROBS.

One is debt. One is equity. Choosing wrong creates strain from day one. 🧵

SBA 7(a) loans:

Government-backed bank financing. Typically 10–20% down as of 2026. Repay over 10 years.

Upside: real capital access, retirement savings stay untouched.

Cost: fixed monthly payments regardless of business performance, 680+ credit required, personal guarantee.

ROBS:

Roll eligible retirement funds into a C-Corp that buys your franchise. No tax event, no early withdrawal penalty, no loan.

Upside: no debt service, no monthly payment.

Cost: your retirement savings are the risk. C-Corp compliance required.

The combined structure:

ROBS covers the SBA down payment. SBA covers the remainder. Lower debt service, retirement funds partially diversified. More common than most buyers realize.

The decision framework:

Strong credit + liquid capital + want retirement savings protected → SBA
Substantial retirement savings + want to avoid debt service → ROBS
Need down payment capital but want smaller loan → ROBS + SBA combined

```

```
Owner-dependent revenue.
```
