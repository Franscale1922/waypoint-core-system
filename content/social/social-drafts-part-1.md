```
→ Willingness to say no when something doesn't fit
→ Honest about what they don't know
```

### The True Cost of Buying a Franchise
**URL:** `/resources/the-true-cost-of-buying-a-franchise`

**Twitter/X Thread:**
```
The number most people see first when researching franchises is the franchise fee.

It is almost never the number that matters.

Here's the full cost picture — 4 numbers most people only know 1 of. 🧵

The franchise fee is typically $40K–$60K for an established brand.

That is a fraction of what it actually costs to open.

The real number depends on what you're building:

Brick-and-mortar (fitness, food, retail): $300K–$800K+ all-in
Non-brick-and-mortar (home services, B2B, restoration): $150K–$350K all-in

That's the total investment. Item 7 of the FDD breaks it down.

Four things that move your number:

1/ How involved you plan to be

Running it yourself = lower starting payroll. Hiring a manager from day one = more working capital needed upfront.

2/ How many territories you're buying

Multi-territory costs more upfront but doesn't scale linearly. Do the math before you start — not after.

3/ Launch marketing spend

Cutting corners here in the first 90 days is one of the most common mistakes I see. Your pipeline IS your business.

4/ Working capital reserve

Whatever you think you'll need — add to it.

The timeline to positive cash flow is almost never exactly what buyers project. Being undercapitalized at a slow month leads to bad decisions fast.

The good news:

$400K doesn't mean $400K in cash.

Most buyers use SBA 7(a) loans (typically 15–20% down) or ROBS (rollover from 401K/IRA, no penalties). Both are real paths.

Minimum practical profile I work with: $100K liquid, $250K net worth. Real options from there.
```

### FDD Decoded: The Items That Matter
**URL:** `/resources/fdd-decoded-what-actually-matters`

**Twitter/X Thread:**
```
The Franchise Disclosure Document is 200–400 pages of legal text.

Most buyers either skip to the financials or hand it to a lawyer and wait.

Both are mistakes. Here are the items that actually matter. 🧵

First: you don't have to wait for a franchisor to give you the FDD.

The Wisconsin Department of Financial Institutions has one of the most complete public FDD databases.

Pull the document before you ever raise your hand. Gives you information before you're in a sales process.

Item 1: Is this business what it says it is?

Corporate history, ownership structure, rebrandings.

Simple = good. If Item 1 gives you a headache, pay attention to that.

Item 2: Who are you getting into business with?

Look for operators, not just salespeople. People who've built and scaled systems.

This is the support team you'll be calling when things get hard.

Items 5, 6, and 7: The real cost picture

5 = franchise fee + terms
6 = ongoing fee structure (royalties, marketing fund, tech fees)
7 = total investment range to open

Plan for your first unit to cost more than the top of Item 7. Not because the numbers are wrong — because things are always harder the first time.

Item 12: How much territory do you get?

Understand exactly how it's defined. Geography vs. population threshold vs. both.

If you want multiple units — does the territory structure even allow that?

Item 19: The financial performance section

Item 19 gets all the attention. It's also the most misread.

Averages across 200 units mean something different than averages across 20 units in 3 metro markets.

Item 19 is the starting point, not the answer.

Item 20: The one I go to first

This is what most buyers barely read.

Two things: system-wide closure counts AND a contact list of every current franchisee.

Call people from that list yourself. Pick names — especially those 2–3 years in, past the honeymoon period.

Ask what a bad week looks like. What they wish they'd known. Whether they'd do it again.
```

### How Franchise Funding Actually Works
**URL:** `/resources/how-franchise-funding-actually-works`

**Twitter/X Thread:**
```
Most people assume franchise ownership requires a cash check.

It doesn't.

Here's how franchise funding actually works — the 3 main paths and how to think about which fits you. 🧵

Path 1: SBA 7(a) Loan

Government-backed. Most common funding path for franchise buyers.

Typically requires 15–20% down as of 2026. On a $400K investment, that's $60K–$80K out of pocket.

The brand matters. SBA lenders who've worked with a specific franchise before know the financials and move faster. Your advisor should connect you with lenders who know the system.

Path 2: ROBS (Rollover for Business Startups)

Lets you invest funds from a 401(k) or IRA into your business.

No early withdrawal penalties. No tax event at rollover.

Often combined with an SBA loan to cover the equity requirement without touching liquid savings.

Path 3: Cash

The simplest path. Most buyers don't write a full check.

But if you have the capital and the right concept, cash gives you the cleanest deal — no debt service from day one.

What actually matters most: understanding your stack before you're in a franchise sales process.

The moment you express serious interest, they're tracking your progress. Not knowing how you're funding it creates a credibility gap at exactly the wrong moment.

Full piece: [link]
```

### The Semi-Absentee Franchise: Real Talk
**URL:** `/resources/the-semi-absentee-franchise-real-talk`

**Twitter/X Thread:**
```
"Semi-absentee franchise" is the term that sells a lot of concepts to a lot of people who end up surprised by what they actually bought.

Let's be honest about what it means — and what it doesn't. 🧵

Semi-absentee means you hire a manager to run the day-to-day. You work 10–15 hours per week, typically focused on oversight and local marketing.

What it doesn't mean:

It doesn't mean hands-off.

It doesn't mean passive income.

Not in year one. Probably not in year two.

What it actually requires:

- More working capital upfront (funding a manager's salary before revenue stabilizes)
- A hiring process that works fast — your business can't open without the right manager
- The operational discipline to manage someone who runs your business
- A realistic 18–24 month runway before the model runs the way you imagined it

Who does well with this model:

People who manage people well and don't need to be in the operation to stay engaged. Corporate managers and executives tend to transfer skills directly.

People who struggle:

People who expected "passive" and didn't budget for a manager's salary from day one. Or people who underestimated how long it takes to hire the right person.

The model is real. The opportunity is real.

Just go in knowing what it actually requires.
```

```
Who pays for it: insurance.

That's the key driver that makes this category different from almost any other.

The investment side:

Capital-heavier than most home services models. The equipment is expensive. Vehicles. Specialized drying and remediation gear.

Total investment typically $200K–$400K depending on the brand and how much equipment you start with.

But the margin is strong when the model is working. Revenue per job is significant.
```
