# Fitness and Wellness Franchise Readiness Checklist

**Kelsey Stuart | Waypoint Franchise Advisors | waypointfranchise.com**

---

This is the checklist I use before any first conversation with someone seriously looking at a fitness or wellness franchise. The category has genuinely strong business models in it. It also has more trendy, poorly structured concepts than almost any other category in franchising. These questions are designed to help you figure out which you are looking at, and whether you are built for it regardless.

---

## Section 1: Financial Readiness

- [ ] Do you have liquid capital for the total investment (typically $100,000 to $600,000 depending on format) plus reserves for the ramp-up period?
- [ ] Have you budgeted at least 12 to 18 months of personal living expenses as a reserve, given that fitness concepts typically take longer than most categories to reach stable membership levels?
- [ ] Have you reviewed Item 7 (the section of the franchise disclosure document that details the total estimated investment required to open, typically $100,000 to $600,000 depending on format as of 2026) and mapped every line item, including build-out, equipment, deposits, and pre-opening marketing?
- [ ] Do you understand that lease obligations in fitness are typically long-term commitments (5 to 10 years) that do not go away if membership growth is slower than projected?
- [ ] If financing through SBA or another vehicle, have you confirmed qualification with a franchise-specialist lender?

---

## Section 2: Understanding the Membership Model

- [ ] Do you understand that fitness franchise revenue is driven by membership retention, not just sign-ups, and that high churn can make a busy-looking studio financially fragile?
- [ ] Have you looked at Item 19 (the section where franchisors may voluntarily disclose financial performance data for existing units) and calculated revenue per member per year, not just average unit volume?
- [ ] Have you asked existing franchisees what their annual churn rate looks like and compared it to industry norms for the model type?
- [ ] Do you understand the difference between a high-volume low-price membership model (high churn, volume-dependent) and a premium small-group model (low churn, pricing power)?
- [ ] Do you understand that the concept you are evaluating may be capturing a trend rather than a durable demand, and have you pressure-tested that distinction?

---

## Section 3: Location and Market Fit

- [ ] Have you verified that your target trade area has sufficient population density and the right consumer demographic for this specific concept?
- [ ] Do you understand how location-dependent fitness businesses are, and that a good concept in a bad location very rarely recovers?
- [ ] Has your proposed site been evaluated against the franchisor's site selection criteria, not just your intuition about the area?
- [ ] Have you looked at what competing fitness concepts operate within your territory and assessed whether there is meaningful white space for this brand?

---

## Section 4: Staffing and Operational Fit

- [ ] Are you comfortable managing fitness instructors, coaches, or wellness practitioners, who often have strong opinions about how they work?
- [ ] Do you understand that instructor availability and retention is a primary operational risk, particularly in boutique models where specific staff drive member loyalty?
- [ ] Have you thought through what happens operationally if a key instructor or practitioner leaves, and whether the brand has systems to manage through that?
- [ ] Are you prepared to be operationally involved in the business for at least the first year, even if your long-term plan is to step back?

---

## Section 5: Lifestyle Fit

- [ ] Does the community and environment of this concept genuinely fit who you are, beyond just finding it interesting as a business?
- [ ] Are you comfortable with the physical environment of running a fitness or wellness location, including the hours, the energy, and the demographic you will be serving?
- [ ] Have you spent meaningful time inside this concept as a participant or observer, not just in a discovery day presentation?
- [ ] Have you thought about what this business looks like in year four when the initial excitement of opening has passed?

---

## Section 6: Franchisor Due Diligence

- [ ] Have you read the full franchise disclosure document, not just the summary from the franchisor or broker?
- [ ] Have you reviewed Item 20 (the section that shows how many franchisees have left the system, and how recently) to understand how many units have closed or been transferred in the last three years?
- [ ] Have you spoken directly with at least five current franchisees selected from the franchise disclosure document's franchisee list, not the franchisor's hand-picked references?
- [ ] Have you asked those franchisees directly what their membership retention looks like compared to their first-year projections?
- [ ] Have you spoken with a franchise attorney who reviewed this specific franchise disclosure document before signing anything?

---

## Scoring Guide

Count how many boxes you checked.

**22 to 24: Strong position.** You understand the category and have done real preparation. Let's talk about specific concepts and how to pressure-test the ones you are evaluating.

**16 to 21: Mostly ready, with some gaps.** The unchecked items are worth addressing before moving forward. Membership model and churn understanding gaps are particularly important in this category.

**10 to 15: More work to do.** The fitness category rewards buyers who go in with clear eyes on revenue structure and location risk. Address the gaps before committing to a process.

**Fewer than 10: Not ready for this conversation yet.** Come back when more of these are yes. The checklist tells you exactly what to work on.
