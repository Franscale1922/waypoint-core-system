Most franchise buyers have two paths to funding without liquidating personal savings: an SBA loan or ROBS. The mechanics are completely different — one is debt, the other is equity — and picking the wrong one for your situation creates financial strain that follows you from day one.

The choice between SBA and ROBS comes down to four things: how much capital you have in retirement accounts, your risk tolerance for debt service, your operational income during the ramp-up period, and how long you intend to own the business. Here's how to think through each.

---

## Side-by-Side: SBA Loan vs. ROBS

| Dimension | SBA Loan | ROBS |
|---|---|---|
| **Type of capital** | Debt (you repay with interest) | Equity (your own retirement funds) |
| **Monthly obligation** | Fixed loan payments begin immediately | No debt service — no monthly payment |
| **Qualification requirements** | Credit score, collateral, business plan | Sufficient retirement account balance (typically $50K+ to be practical) |
| **Tax implications** | Interest is deductible; no upfront tax event | No tax penalty at rollover; ongoing C-Corp compliance required |
| **Business structure required** | Any entity type | C-Corporation only |
| **Setup complexity** | Bank underwriting process (6–12 weeks as of 2026) | Requires a ROBS administrator; typically 3–4 weeks |
| **Best for** | Buyers with strong credit, limited retirement savings, steady income for servicing debt | Buyers with substantial retirement accounts, limited liquid capital, preference to avoid debt |
| **Risk profile** | If the business underperforms, debt service continues regardless | If the business fails, retirement funds are lost — no repayment required, but the capital is gone |

---

## The SBA Path: When It Works and When It Doesn't

The SBA 7(a) loan program is the most common franchise funding tool in the US. SBA doesn't lend directly — it guarantees loans made by participating banks, which reduces lender risk and makes loans available to buyers who wouldn't otherwise qualify for commercial financing.

**What SBA requires.** Credit score minimums vary by lender, but 680 is a practical floor as of 2026 for most franchise deals. You'll need a down payment of 10–20% of the total project cost in liquid capital. Collateral is often required — typically your home equity or business assets if the loan exceeds $25,000. Personal guarantee is standard.

**What SBA funds.** Most franchise purchases qualify for SBA financing because the SBA maintains a Franchise Registry — a list of approved franchise systems. If your target brand is on the registry, underwriting moves faster. If it isn't, you can still get SBA financing, but it requires additional documentation.

**The debt service reality.** A $300,000 SBA loan at prevailing rates as of 2026 carries a monthly payment of roughly $2,800–$3,200 over a 10-year term. That payment begins whether or not your business has ramped up. In a franchise with a 6–12 month ramp-up period before cash flow stabilizes, you need reserves to cover that payment during the build phase. Factor this into your total capital requirement.

**SBA works best when:** you have good credit, at least 15–20% of the total investment in liquid capital, a realistic picture of debt service during ramp-up, and enough external income to cover personal expenses for 12 months while the business gets established.

## The ROBS Path: When It Works and When It Doesn't

ROBS — Rollover for Business Startups — lets you move money from a qualifying retirement account (401(k), IRA, 403(b)) into your new franchise without paying early-withdrawal taxes or penalties. The structure requires forming a C-Corporation, having that corporation adopt a retirement plan, then rolling the retirement funds into that plan, which purchases stock in your company.

The funds in your business are now equity. There is no debt. There is no monthly payment. If the business runs out of money, the funds are gone — but you owe nothing.

**What ROBS requires.** A qualifying retirement account with enough balance to be meaningful. In practice, ROBS only makes financial sense if you have $50,000 or more in eligible accounts, and typically works best with $100,000+. The structure requires forming a C-Corporation, which has specific tax implications (C-Corps are taxed at the entity level, unlike S-Corps or LLCs). Ongoing compliance — annual filings, retirement plan administration — is handled by a ROBS administrator and costs $1,500–$3,000 per year as of 2026.

**The structural constraint.** C-Corporation structure is non-negotiable. Many franchise agreements are written to accommodate this, but confirm with your attorney before committing to a brand.

**The retirement account risk.** ROBS doesn't create debt, but it redirects retirement savings into a single business investment. If the business performs poorly, that capital does not come back. This is a material consideration for buyers approaching retirement age or those whose franchise funding represents all or most of their retirement savings. Concentration risk is real.

**ROBS works best when:** you have a meaningful retirement account balance, you want to avoid monthly debt service during the ramp-up period, you plan to operate the business for at least five years (long enough to rebuild retirement savings), and you're comfortable with the ongoing compliance requirements.

---

## The Combined Structure: Using Both

Many buyers use ROBS to cover the SBA down-payment requirement, then take an SBA loan for the remainder.

**How this works:** ROBS provides 20% of the total project cost as equity (satisfying the SBA down payment requirement), SBA provides the remaining 80% as a loan. The buyer's monthly debt service is lower than a fully SBA-funded deal because the total loan amount is smaller. The retirement account risk is also lower because only the down-payment portion is tied up in ROBS.

The combined structure requires coordination between the ROBS administrator and the SBA lender. Not all lenders are comfortable with this structure, so find a lender who works with ROBS regularly before you start the process.

## The Decision Framework

One question drives the choice: how much capital do you have, and where is it?

| Situation | Recommended Path |
|---|---|
| Strong credit, liquid savings for down payment, monthly income during ramp-up | SBA loan |
| Substantial retirement savings, want to avoid debt service | ROBS |
| Retirement savings but not enough liquid capital for SBA down payment | ROBS + SBA combined |
| Limited retirement savings, limited liquid capital | Address capital position before proceeding |

If your answer to that last row is your situation, stop and address it before going further. The biggest mistake in franchise funding isn't picking the wrong structure — it's undercapitalizing the purchase.

## Common Questions

**What is the difference between an SBA loan and ROBS for franchise funding?**

An SBA loan is a government-backed bank loan that you repay with interest over time, typically 10 years for franchise funding as of 2026. ROBS — Rollover for Business Startups — is a legal structure that lets you invest retirement funds directly into your new franchise business without triggering early-withdrawal penalties or taxes. SBA creates debt; ROBS creates equity. They solve the same cash problem in structurally opposite ways.

**How much of a down payment does an SBA 7(a) loan require for a franchise?**

SBA 7(a) loans for franchise funding typically require 10–20% of the total project cost as a down payment as of 2026. If your total investment is $300,000, expect to inject $30,000–$60,000 in liquid capital. The remainder is borrowed. Credit score minimums, collateral requirements, and repayment terms vary by lender — SBA sets the program parameters, but individual banks underwrite the loan.

**Is ROBS legal and IRS-approved?**

ROBS is legal. The IRS has published formal guidance on the structure and has reviewed it extensively. That said, ROBS must be set up correctly by a qualified administrator — usually a specialist firm — and the ongoing compliance requirements are real. Done correctly, it is a legitimate and widely used funding path. Done incorrectly, it creates significant tax and legal exposure.

**Can you use both SBA and ROBS to fund a franchise?**

Yes. Many buyers use a combined structure where ROBS covers the down payment requirement and an SBA loan provides the remainder. This eliminates the need for liquid personal savings for the down payment while keeping monthly debt service lower than a fully SBA-funded deal. The combined approach requires careful coordination between the ROBS administrator and the SBA lender.
