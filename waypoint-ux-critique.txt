WAYPOINTFRANCHISE.COMConversion Rate Optimization & UX CritiquePrepared March 2026 | Independent Review, No Prior Knowledge of SiteExecutive SummaryThis site is significantly better than 90% of franchise consultant websites. The copy is strong. The voice feels real. The differentiation (fiduciary model, 70% don't buy stat, personal failure story) is legitimate and well deployed. The photography of Montana and Kelsey himself builds real trust.That said, the site has problems that are costing conversions. They fall into two categories: structural issues that create friction in the visitor's decision path, and missed opportunities where the existing content is good but not working hard enough.The biggest single issue: the site does not give skeptical, analytical visitors enough concrete proof to bridge the gap between 'this seems interesting' and 'I should call this guy.' The testimonials are solid in tone but structurally weak. There is no third party validation anywhere. And the site never shows what a successful outcome actually looks like in enough detail to make the risk feel manageable.Below is a page by page breakdown, findings ranked by conversion impact within each page, followed by the Top 5 Global Issues.
Homepage (waypointfranchise.com)First Impression (0 to 3 seconds)The hero does its job. 'Find the franchise that fits your life' is clear positioning. The subhead introduces Kelsey by name, establishes the service, and addresses cost in the first sentence. The mountain lake image sets a specific, non-corporate mood. This is above average for the category.Problem: 'Former franchise owner' in the subhead is doing less work than it should. It blurs together Kelsey's franchisor experience (built $40M brand) and his franchisee experience (failed and lost money). A visitor scanning in three seconds gets 'this guy owned a franchise' but not the more powerful story underneath it.Findings, Ranked by Conversion Impact1. The testimonials look fabricated. This is the single biggest conversion killer on the homepage. Every testimonial follows the same pattern: first name, last initial, city, and a 'Readiness Score' number. No photos. No last names. No LinkedIn links. No company names. No video. For an audience of VP/Director level professionals making a six figure decision, this reads like AI generated content. These people know what real endorsements look like because they deal with them at work every day. The Readiness Score numbers actually make it worse because they reference an internal tool the visitor has not taken yet, so they feel like a prop rather than proof.Ą Fix: Get real photos, real full names (with permission), real job titles. If people will not give permission for their full identity, get video testimonials where they speak on camera. Even three genuine video clips would replace eight text blocks and be 10x more credible. Kill the Readiness Score from the testimonials entirely.2. No proof of outcomes beyond 'helped.' The site says '146+ owners helped across 35 states.' Helped doing what? What happened to them? A skeptical executive wants to know: What did these people's businesses actually do after they opened? Are they making money? Are they happy? The site never bridges from 'I matched them' to 'here is what their life looks like now.' The testimonials say things like 'best decision I've made' but never once mention a number, a result, or a timeline. This audience thinks in outcomes, not feelings.Ą Fix: Add two or three case study snapshots. They do not need to be full pages. A short block that says something like: 'Marcus left a Regional Director role in 2023. We matched him to a home services franchise in Denver. He opened in Q2 2024. Year one revenue: $480K. He works 40 hours a week and has not missed a soccer game.' That is what converts this audience. Specific, verifiable, result oriented.3. The persona cards ('Burned Out Achiever,' etc.) are emotionally strong but functionally dead. Five persona sections with great copy, and none of them link to anything specific. They all dump into the same two CTAs (Book a Call, Take the Quiz). This is a wasted opportunity. Each persona type has different objections, different timelines, and different information needs. The 3 to 5 Year Planner is not going to book a call today. The Corporate Expat might. Treating them identically flattens the conversion path.Ą Fix: Give each persona a tailored next step. The Planner gets a link to the resources library. The Expat gets a direct 'Book Now' button with urgency copy. The Trailing Spouse gets pointed toward the semi-absentee article. Match the CTA to where each person actually is in their decision process.4. The stats bar ($40M+, 200+ locations, 250+ concepts, $0 cost) is doing heavy work but has a trust gap. $40M and 200 locations are impressive numbers. But they are presented without any source, context, or link. For this audience, unattributed stats on a personal website raise more questions than they answer. Where does $40M come from? Is it audited? Is it revenue or franchise fees? These people verify claims for a living.Ą Fix: Add context. '$40M+ system revenue as Chief Development Officer at Bloomin' Blinds (2015 to 2022)' is more credible than '$40M+' floating in a box. If there is press coverage, an Inc article, a franchise industry ranking, link to it. External validation makes the number real.5. Three CTAs in the hero may be one too many. Book a Free Call, Take the Readiness Quiz, and Or Text Me all appear above the fold. For someone just arriving, this introduces a choice before they have context. The text option is low commitment and good. The quiz is medium commitment. The call is highest. Presenting all three simultaneously creates a micro moment of 'which one should I click?' that slightly increases the chance they click nothing.Ą Fix: Test reducing to two options in the hero: the quiz (low friction entry) and text/call (for people ready now). Move the 'Book a Free Call' button to appear after the testimonial section or process preview, once the visitor has enough context to commit to 30 minutes.6. The page is long and the mid-section loses momentum. The homepage goes: hero, stats, 'let's be honest' section, five persona cards, process preview, eight testimonials, about preview, final CTA. That is a lot of scrolling. The five persona cards especially create a long visual block that a return visitor will skip and a first time visitor may not finish. By the time someone reaches the testimonials they have already scrolled through a significant amount of content without being given a conversion moment.Ą Fix: Add a sticky or floating CTA that appears after the first scroll. Even a small 'Book a Free Call' bar at the top of the screen that slides in after the hero scrolls out of view. Do not let the visitor forget the primary action while they are in the middle of a long page.
About Page (waypointfranchise.com/about)First ImpressionStrong. The headline 'I am Kelsey Stuart' is direct. The subtext names Bloomin' Blinds, establishes the franchisor background, and delivers the core promise (honesty about whether franchise ownership is the right move) in one sentence. The fly fishing hero image reinforces the Montana identity. Good page.Findings, Ranked by Conversion Impact7. No third party validation anywhere. Kelsey references Bloomin' Blinds, FranChoice affiliation, $40M, 200+ locations. All of this is self reported. There is no FranChoice logo or badge. No link to a Bloomin' Blinds press mention. No industry award, certification, podcast appearance, or media feature. Nothing external confirms that Kelsey is who he says he is. For a skeptical executive researching a six figure decision, this gap matters a lot. They will Google him anyway. Make it easy for them to find validation on the site itself.Ą Fix: Add a small 'As seen in' or 'Affiliated with' section. FranChoice logo. Bloomin' Blinds logo. Any podcast appearances, franchise industry press, speaking engagements, or LinkedIn post that went viral. Even a screenshot of a LinkedIn recommendation from a known franchise executive would help.8. The credentials table is good but undersells the story. The table format (Role, Network, Location, etc.) is clean, but it presents Kelsey's background as a resume instead of as proof. 'Franchisee background: Operated a franchise unit; experienced failure and financial loss' is honest but reads like a LinkedIn field. The power of this story is that Kelsey knows what it feels like to fail with real money on the line, and that is exactly why he is good at keeping other people from doing the same thing. The table compresses that into a single row.Ą Fix: The table is fine for quick scanning. But add a short paragraph below it that tells the franchisee failure story with one or two specific details. What was the franchise? How much did you lose? What did you learn that you now use with candidates? That specificity is what separates a real story from a talking point.9. 'Roughly 7 in 10 people I work with decide not to buy a franchise' is the best line on the entire site. It is buried. This stat is a conversion weapon. It is the single most trust building data point on the site because it proves Kelsey is not a salesperson. It sits inside a blockquote on the About page. It should be one of the first things a visitor sees, on the homepage, not three scrolls deep on a secondary page.Ą Fix: Put this stat on the homepage, possibly in the hero area or immediately below it. Frame it as a differentiator: '70% of the people I work with do not end up buying a franchise. That is how you know I am not selling you something.' This alone could move the needle on call bookings.
Process Page (waypointfranchise.com/process)First ImpressionThis is the strongest page on the site. The 'Two hours before you see a single brand' headline immediately sets expectations and differentiates from every other franchise consultant. The five step walkthrough is clear, specific, and written in a human voice. The chauffeur analogy in Step 5 is memorable. The 'What you need to get started' section at the bottom is exactly the right amount of pre-qualification.Findings, Ranked by Conversion Impact10. No timeline expectation. The process page explains what happens at each step but never tells the visitor how long the whole thing takes. For someone who just got laid off, that matters a lot. For a 3 to 5 year planner, it also matters. 'Intro call to franchise agreement: typical timeline is 60 to 120 days' would give people a sense of the commitment they are signing up for. Without it, the process feels open ended, which creates anxiety in analytical people.Ą Fix: Add a simple timeline indicator. Even a 'Most candidates move from first call to final decision in 60 to 90 days, though there is no pressure on timing' line at the top or bottom of the steps.11. The mid-page CTA placement is awkward. After Step 4 (Validation Calls), there is a 'Book a 30 minute intro call' CTA box. Then Step 5 (The Decision) continues below it. This breaks the narrative flow. The visitor has not finished reading the process, and you are asking them to commit. It also implies that Steps 4 and 5 are less important than Steps 1 through 3 because they are visually separated by a conversion element.Ą Fix: Move the mid-page CTA to after Step 5. Let the full process play out, then ask for the commitment. Or use a more subtle in-line prompt between steps ('Sound like the right approach? Keep reading, or book a call now.') that does not break the visual narrative.12. The 'What you need to get started' section is perfect but should include one more item: 'A spouse or partner who is at least open to the conversation.' Kelsey's audience includes married professionals making a family level financial decision. One of the biggest reasons someone leaves a site like this without booking is 'I need to talk to my wife/husband first.' Acknowledging that up front, and normalizing it, reduces the friction of booking a call solo.Ą Fix: Add a fifth requirement: something like 'Your partner does not need to be on the first call. But if this moves forward, they will be part of the conversation. That is normal and I encourage it.'
FAQ Page (waypointfranchise.com/faq)First ImpressionComprehensive and well organized. Eight categories, good anchor links at the top. The questions are phrased the way a real person would ask them, not the way a marketer would write them. 'Can I keep my job while the franchise gets started?' is exactly the question someone is Googling at 11pm.Findings, Ranked by Conversion Impact13. The FAQ answers are collapsed (accordion) and I cannot see them from the fetch. This is both a UX note and an SEO note. If the answers are hidden behind JavaScript accordions that are not rendered in the initial HTML, search engines may not index them. Google can render JavaScript, but it is inconsistent. For AEO/AI citation purposes, this is a real problem because AI tools typically cannot expand accordions. The FAQ content could be the most cited page on the site if the answers are visible in the HTML.Ą Fix: Ensure FAQ answers are in the initial HTML, not loaded on click. Use structured data markup (FAQPage schema). Test by loading the page with JavaScript disabled and confirming all answers are visible. This is a high value SEO/AEO fix.14. The FAQ page has no inline CTAs. Someone reading about franchise funding, legal agreements, or whether they need a consultant is in active research mode. They are close to making a decision about whether to engage. Every FAQ section should end with a contextual nudge. 'Still have questions about funding? That is one of the first things we cover on a call.' instead of making the visitor scroll back to the top or bottom to find a Book a Call button.Ą Fix: Add a contextual CTA after every 3 to 4 questions, or at the end of each section. Match the CTA to the topic. Funding questions get a CTA about discussing capital. Legal questions get a CTA about reviewing the FDD together.
Investment Page (waypointfranchise.com/investment)First ImpressionThis page is excellent. It is the kind of page that builds serious trust with analytical buyers. The cost breakdown table is specific. The investment tiers section is practical and maps capital to business types. The financing section covers SBA, ROBS, and HELOC with enough detail to be useful but not enough to overwhelm. The FAQ at the bottom is well integrated. This page will rank well and get shared.Findings, Ranked by Conversion Impact15. No calculator or interactive element. This audience will mentally run numbers while reading this page. A simple 'What can I afford?' calculator that takes liquid capital and shows the investment tier, estimated financing needs, and likely franchise categories would dramatically increase time on page and provide a natural bridge to booking a call. It also creates a data exchange moment where the visitor has invested effort and received personalized output, which makes the next step (talking to Kelsey) feel like a continuation rather than a cold start.Ą Fix: Add an interactive investment range calculator at the top or bottom of the page. Input: liquid capital available. Output: tier category, example franchise types, estimated total investment range, and a CTA to 'Talk through your specific situation.'16. The cost breakdown table is duplicated. The page shows the same cost data twice: once as styled cards and once as a markdown table. This looks like a rendering issue or an intentional mobile/desktop choice, but in the fetched HTML both are present. If both show on desktop, it creates visual noise and makes the visitor question whether the page was QA'd properly.Ą Fix: Verify the desktop and mobile rendering. If one is meant to be hidden on desktop, confirm the CSS is working. If both are visible, pick one and remove the other.
Resources Page (waypointfranchise.com/resources)First ImpressionImpressive library. The article titles are strong and written as real questions a buyer would search for. The three category system (Getting Started, Going Deeper, Industry Spotlights) is a clean information architecture. The 'Start here' section at the top provides an obvious entry point for new visitors. This page is a real asset.Findings, Ranked by Conversion Impact17. No content upgrades, lead magnets, or email capture anywhere in the resources section. Someone who reads three articles on franchise investment, FDD analysis, and senior care is a highly qualified lead. Right now, they read the content and either book a call or leave. There is no middle ground. For the 3 to 5 Year Planner persona, the middle ground is essential because they are not ready for a call but they are ready to be nurtured. The site has no way to keep that relationship warm.Ą Fix: Create a single, high value PDF (something like 'The 10 Questions Every Franchise Buyer Should Ask Before Signing') and gate it behind an email. Place it as a contextual offer within the article flow, not as a popup. This builds an email list of qualified prospects without requiring a call commitment.18. No reading time estimates on articles. This audience values their time. A '6 min read' tag on each card signals that the content is substantive but not a time sink. It also implicitly says 'we respect your schedule,' which aligns with the brand.Ą Fix: Add estimated read times to each article card.19. No search results state or filtering UX visible. There is a search bar at the top ('Search articles') which is good. But with 20+ articles, the page is already long. Adding category filters that work as toggles (so someone can see only 'Going Deeper' articles without scrolling past 'Getting Started') would reduce browsing friction.Ą Fix: Make the category headings work as sticky filter tabs at the top of the article grid.
Contact Page (waypointfranchise.com/contact)First ImpressionClean and functional. The form is short (name, email, phone optional, message). The alternative contact options (call, text, email) are clearly presented. The 'responding Mon to Fri' note sets expectations. No complaints about the basics.Findings, Ranked by Conversion Impact20. The contact page and the book page compete with each other. Both are in the navigation. Both have forms. Both lead to Kelsey. For a visitor who lands on the contact page, should they fill out the form or go book a call? This creates decision friction. The contact page exists for a different intent ('I have a question but I am not ready to talk') but the visual weight of the form makes it feel like the primary conversion path when it should be the secondary one.Ą Fix: Make the contact page more clearly a 'not ready for a call yet' option. Reduce the form to just name and message. Add a stronger visual push toward the Book page with language like 'If you are ready to talk, skip the form and grab a time directly.'21. No response time promise. 'I will get back to you within one business day' is stated but could be stronger. 'You will hear back from me personally within 24 hours. Not a bot, not an assistant. Me.' reinforces the solo advisor positioning and builds trust in the channel.
Book Page (waypointfranchise.com/book)First ImpressionThis page is the most important page on the site and it is the weakest. The hero image and Kelsey's photo are good. The bullet points ('No brands shown on the first call,' 'Real conversation, not a pitch,' 'Honest answer, even if it's no') do their job. But the actual booking mechanism is either missing from the rendered page or embedded in a way that does not appear in the HTML. If the Calendly or scheduling widget is loading via JavaScript that failed to render, that is a critical conversion failure.Findings, Ranked by Conversion Impact22. The booking calendar may not be rendering for all visitors. When I fetched this page, there was no visible calendar or scheduling widget in the HTML. If this is a JavaScript embedded calendar (Calendly, Cal.com, etc.), there are scenarios where it fails to load: slow connections, JavaScript blockers, corporate proxy networks. For an audience that may be browsing from a corporate laptop with restricted browser settings, this is a real risk. The page should have a fallback.Ą Fix: Add a visible fallback below the calendar widget area. Something like: 'Calendar not loading? Text me at (214) 995-1062 or email kelsey@waypointfranchise.com and I will send you a link.' This catches the visitors who cannot access the widget.23. No objection handling on the booking page. The visitor has made it to the finish line. They are looking at the calendar. This is the moment where last second objections spike. 'Is he going to try to sell me something?' 'What if I am not far enough along?' 'Will he judge me for not knowing anything about franchising?' The page addresses none of these. A single FAQ accordion or a set of three short reassurances below the calendar could catch the visitor who is hovering over the book button but has not clicked.Ą Fix: Add a small 'What to expect' section directly below the calendar: 'I will not pitch you anything. I will not ask you to commit to anything. We will talk for 30 minutes about where you are and whether franchise ownership is worth exploring further. About 70% of the people I talk to decide it is not. That is a perfectly fine outcome.'24. No social proof on the booking page. This is the one page where a single strong testimonial could tip the balance. Not eight testimonials. One. Specifically from someone who almost did not book the call and is glad they did. 'I sat on this page for a week before I booked. Best decision I made.' That kind of proof at the point of conversion is extremely high leverage.
Top 5 Global IssuesRanked by estimated conversion impact, highest first.1. The Proof GapThe number one reason a qualified candidate leaves this site without contacting Kelsey is that the evidence does not match the claim. The site makes a bold claim: 'I am different from other franchise consultants. I will tell you not to buy. 70% of my candidates do not buy.' That is a powerful differentiator. But the proof architecture supporting it is weak. The testimonials have no faces, no full names, and no verifiable details. There are no case studies. There is no third party validation. No press mentions. No industry affiliations displayed. No video of Kelsey being Kelsey.For the target audience, every other vendor they evaluate comes with LinkedIn endorsements, case studies, reference calls, and press coverage. This site asks them to trust the copy itself. The copy is good. But copy alone does not close a skeptical executive making a six figure decision. Fix this first. Everything else is secondary.2. No Middle of Funnel CaptureThe site offers two actions: book a call (high commitment) or take the quiz (medium commitment). There is nothing for the person who is six months out, still employed, browsing at night, and not ready to talk to anyone. That person reads three articles, finds them useful, and then leaves. The site has no email capture, no newsletter, no downloadable guide, no 'get notified when I publish new content' option. This is a large audience segment (the 3 to 5 Year Planner persona) that the site acknowledges but cannot convert.Fix: Create one gated content asset (PDF guide, email series, or a monthly newsletter) that captures an email address from the not-ready-yet visitor. A five part email series called 'Five Things to Know Before You Talk to Any Franchise Consultant' would be perfect for this audience and perfectly aligned with the brand.3. The Testimonials Need a Complete OverhaulEvery testimonial on the site follows the same format: quote, first name last initial, city, one line bio, and a Readiness Score. They read like templates. Whether they are real or not does not matter because they do not feel real. In CRO, perception is the only thing that matters.Fix: Replace or supplement with: (a) Two to three 60 second video testimonials from real candidates, shot on a phone, unscripted. (b) Full name, photo, and LinkedIn link for text testimonials (with permission). (c) At least one case study with specific financial outcomes. (d) Remove the Readiness Score from all testimonials because it references an internal metric that means nothing to a visitor. If you can only do one thing from this entire document, do the video testimonials.4. The Site Does Not Guide Return VisitorsA franchise decision is not made in one visit. This audience comes back two, three, five times before they act. The site treats every visit as if it is the first one. There is no 'welcome back' behavior. There is no suggested reading path. There is no 'where you left off.' The homepage and every interior page show the same CTAs regardless of what the visitor has already read. This is a missed opportunity for a site with 20+ articles of strong content.Fix: This is a longer term fix. Consider implementing a reading progress tracker or a simple quiz result page that persists. At minimum, the resources page should have a clear recommended reading order ('Start here, then read this, then this') that gives return visitors a sense of progression.5. Mobile Experience Needs Sticky CTA TreatmentThe pages are long. On mobile, where the target audience is likely doing initial research (browsing on the couch, on a plane, in bed at 11pm), scrolling through the homepage means losing sight of the primary CTA for extended periods. The text link in the nav is small. There is no sticky bottom bar with a call to action. On mobile, the easiest conversion path should be a one tap 'Text Kelsey' button that stays visible at all times, because texting is the lowest friction action on a phone.Fix: Add a sticky mobile bottom bar with two options: 'Text Kelsey' and 'Book a Call.' This is standard CRO practice for mobile service sites and it reliably increases conversion rates. Keep it subtle (thin bar, not a full banner) so it does not feel pushy.
Final AssessmentThis site is built on a genuinely strong foundation. The voice is real. The story is powerful. The copy quality is well above the category average. The Montana photography and personal images make it feel like a real person's business, not a template. The resources library is a serious content asset that will compound over time.The problems are fixable and mostly center on one theme: the site tells a great story but does not prove it. An executive evaluating a $300K life decision needs more than good writing. They need faces, names, numbers, outcomes, and external validation. Give them that, and this site will convert significantly better than it does today.Priority order for implementation:25. Get two to three video testimonials from real candidates. This alone changes the trust equation.26. Move the '70% do not buy' stat to the homepage hero area.27. Add third party validation (FranChoice logo, press mentions, LinkedIn proof).28. Create one email capture asset to catch the not-ready-yet visitor.29. Add sticky mobile CTA and objection handling on the /book page.Certainty indicator: 85% factual (based on direct page content analysis), 15% assumption based (mobile rendering, JavaScript widget behavior, visitor behavior patterns drawn from CRO best practices). I did not test load speed, run Lighthouse, or view the actual rendered pages in a browser, so some visual/layout observations may differ from the live experience.
