# Waypoint Franchise Advisors — Brand Voice & Tone Guide

**Version:** 1.0 — April 2026
**Owner:** Kelsey Stuart, Waypoint Franchise Advisors
**Purpose:** Master reference for any AI, writer, or collaborator creating content for Waypoint Franchise Advisors. Read this document before writing anything.

---

## Who Kelsey Is

Kelsey Stuart is a franchise advisor and the owner of Waypoint Franchise Advisors, based in Whitefish, Montana.

His work involves helping professionals evaluate franchise ownership as a possible next chapter. He does not sell franchises. He guides individuals through the process of exploring whether ownership is the right fit for their goals, risk tolerance, and lifestyle.

**Critical positioning:**
- He is a guide, not a salesperson
- He is a fiduciary advisor — his obligation is to the person he is helping, not to any franchise brand
- His service costs the candidate nothing. Franchise brands compensate him only if a candidate awards a franchise, and only after that decision is made independently
- He does not represent any single brand. He works across hundreds of concepts spanning many industries
- Most people who talk to him do not end up buying a franchise — and that is fine with him

## The Audience

All Waypoint content is written for one primary reader: a mid-to-late career professional who has built a strong career working for others and who is quietly wondering if there is something different for them.

**Core audience profile:**
- Age 40–58
- High performer — VP, Director, CXO, senior operator, engineer, finance professional, sales leader
- Has spent most of their career building someone else's company
- May feel stuck, underappreciated, or quietly wondering if this is it
- Not reckless — they think carefully before major decisions
- Has a family, a mortgage, real responsibilities
- Analytically oriented and deeply skeptical of marketing language

**Internal persona name:** "Corporate Refugee" — do not use this phrase in content, but keep this person in mind when writing.

Write to a single person sitting at their desk on a Wednesday afternoon wondering whether this is it. Do not write to a demographic. Do not write to a room.

## Brand Voice

The voice of Waypoint sounds like an experienced professional speaking plainly to another experienced professional.

**Voice characteristics:**
- Calm
- Direct
- Respectful
- Measured
- Warm but not soft
- Confident but not arrogant
- Low pressure — never urgent

The voice should never sound excited, promotional, or enthusiastic. It must not sound like automation. It must not sound scripted.

It treats the reader as an intelligent adult who does not need to be convinced — only invited to think about something they may already be considering.

---

## What Waypoint Content Must Never Do

- Pitch a specific franchise brand
- Promise financial outcomes, income claims, or investment returns
- Describe franchise ownership as a dream, a passion, or a life-changing opportunity
- Create urgency — there are no deadlines, no limited offers
- Diagnose the reader's situation or tell them how they feel
- Use corporate buzzwords, hype language, or empty enthusiasm
- Sound like it was generated by an AI

## Tone by Content Type

### Outbound email / cold outreach
Reserved, observational, low-pressure. One goal: earn a single reply. Not a meeting, not a commitment — just an open door.

### Website copy
Clear and professional. Benefit-first framing. Structured like a confident conversation, not a sales page. Every sentence must earn its place.

### Blog / resource articles
Educational, specific, and authoritative. Avoids generic franchise content. Speaks to the emotional and analytical realities of evaluating a business decision — not just the mechanics. Uses real industry data. Does not fabricate specifics.

### Newsletter (The Franchise Dispatch)
Conversational but substantive. One strong idea per issue, developed with care. Grounded in real audience signals — language drawn from what people actually say when they are wrestling with this decision, not polished marketing speak.

## Writing Style Rules

**Sentence length:** 8–14 words preferred. Vary the rhythm — short punchy sentences after longer ones. Never more than two long sentences in a row.

**Paragraph length:** Two to four sentences maximum. Content must be easy to read on a phone screen.

**Reading level:** Seventh grade or lower. Not because the audience is unsophisticated — because clear, simple writing moves faster and feels more honest than formal prose.

**Word count (email):** 70–140 words. Emails longer than 150 words should be cut.

**Formatting in email:** No bullet points. No em dashes. No exclamation points. No bold text for emphasis inside sentences. Plain text only.

---

## Language Guidelines

Use clear, everyday business language.

**Appropriate language:**
- ownership, operating a business, evaluating options
- risk and tradeoffs, operating models, long-term decisions
- career transition, income structure
- explore, consider, think through
- what it actually looks like, what most people don't know

**Do not use:**
- leverage (as a verb)
- delve, dive deep, dive into
- cutting edge, game changer, game-changing
- seamless, synergy, paradigm, tapestry, realm, foster, catalyst
- navigate/navigating, testament
- optimize, scale rapidly
- passive income, financial freedom
- life-changing opportunity, exclusive opportunity, ground floor opportunity
- revolutionary, disruptive

## Forbidden Openings and Closings

### Never open with:
- "I hope this email finds you well"
- "I wanted to reach out"
- "I am excited to share"
- "I came across your profile"
- "I'm reaching out because"
- "Just touching base" / "checking in"
- Any compliment to the reader
- Kelsey's name or company name

### Never close with:
- "Let me know if you have any questions"
- "Looking forward to connecting"
- "Touch base" / "circle back" / "move the needle"
- "It goes without saying" / "It is worth noting"
- "At the end of the day"
- "In today's landscape" / "in today's world"
- Any variation of "I just wanted to"

### Appropriate closings:
- "If this has ever crossed your mind, happy to compare notes."
- "Feel free to reply if any of this is interesting."
- "No pressure either way."
- "If you have ever thought about this, I would be glad to talk."

## Personalization Rules

Personalization should feel subtle and observational — like a thoughtful person noticed something relevant, not like a system scraped data.

**Acceptable:**
- Referencing the reader's industry or professional function
- Referencing their career stage or general role type
- Referencing the topic of something they published (without fabricating familiarity)

**Never:**
- Fabricate that you know them or have followed their work
- Over-personalize in a way that feels invasive or studied
- Compliment the reader's background, profile, or experience

---

## WIIFM Principle

Every paragraph must answer the reader's implied question: *why does this matter to me?*

Lead with the benefit to the reader before explaining anything about the service or the person providing it. If a sentence is only about Kelsey or Waypoint, cut it or restructure it so the reader is the subject.

---

## Email Structure (When Writing Outbound Outreach)

Follow this sequence:

1. **Open** with something relevant to the reader — not an introduction about Kelsey, not a compliment
2. **Name the moment** they may recognize — without diagnosing or judging them
3. **Introduce Kelsey's role** briefly — one to two sentences, low-risk, no-cost, no-obligation framing
4. **One next step** — never more than one call to action
5. **Close** warmly and without pressure

The opening line must earn the reader's attention immediately.

Content written for Waypoint lives within one of these themes. Each angle should serve the reader, not promote the service.

| Pillar | What it addresses |
|---|---|
| The Decision | The internal experience of evaluating a major career or financial change |
| The Process | What franchise exploration actually looks like — step by step, no hype |

## The Audience Personality Framework (For Content Targeting)

Waypoint uses eight buyer archetypes to shape content angles and personalization. These are behavioral types, not job titles. Each archetype has different motivations, fears, and franchise category fits.

| Archetype | Core Drive | Franchise Category Fit |
|---|---|---|

| The Caregiver | Serving others through genuine connection | Senior care, pet services, children's enrichment |
| The Connector | Relationships as a business engine | Beauty, business services, senior care, real estate |
| The Operator | Systems and process discipline | Home services, restoration, lawn care, food/beverage |
| The Driver | Results through direct action and persuasion | Junk removal, home improvement, pest control, B2B services |

| The Creative | Aesthetics, brand experience, differentiation | Beauty, boutique fitness, entertainment, children's enrichment |

When writing archetype-targeted content, lead with behavioral recognition — what they do, how they think, what frustrates them — not what they should buy.

## Newsletter Voice (The Franchise Dispatch)

The Franchise Dispatch is a weekly newsletter for people who are in the early stages of considering franchise ownership. It is not a sales tool. It is an educational resource.

**Format:**
- Subject line: specific, not clever. The reader should know what they are getting before they open it.
- Body: one strong idea, developed fully. Not a roundup. Not a tip list.
- Tone: the same voice throughout — calm, direct, useful

**Content rotation:**

| Issue | Content Type |
|---|---|
| 1 | Both Sides of the Table (franchisor + buyer perspective) |
| 2 | Myth-Busting |
| 3 | Story-Driven |
| 4 | Educational / Insight |
| 5 | Industry Observation |
| 6 | repeat |

**Core themes (drawn from real audience signals):**
- burnout-escape / salary-fear
- identity and midlife transition
- the franchise evaluation process and what it actually looks like
- capital, risk, and the numbers that actually matter
- analysis paralysis and timing anxiety
- family dynamics and the conversation behind the conversation

**Tone rule:** Use the language the audience uses — not the language a franchise marketer would use. Mine verbatim from real audience sources. Do not polish the edge out of it.

## Final QA Checklist

Before delivering any content:

- [ ] The tone is calm, professional, and unhurried — never excited or promotional
- [ ] No marketing language is present
- [ ] No forbidden words or phrases are present
- [ ] The opening does not compliment the reader
- [ ] Every paragraph passes the WIIFM test
- [ ] If email: word count is 70–140 words
- [ ] If email: sentences are short and varied (8–14 words preferred)
- [ ] If email: exactly one call to action
- [ ] If email: no em dashes, no exclamation points, no bullet points
- [ ] No income claims, brand pitches, or urgency tactics
- [ ] It reads like a real professional wrote it personally

If any check fails, rewrite before delivering.
